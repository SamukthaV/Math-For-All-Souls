Solving a DE describes a subset (a family) of functions; choosing initial data picks one member ‚Äî a particular solution.

What is a differential equation?

A (scalar) ODE of order $n$ relates a function $y(x)$ to its derivatives: \[ F\!\big(x,y,y',\dots,y^{(n)}\big)=0. \] The order is the highest derivative present. If $F$ is linear in $y,\dots,y^{(n)}$, we call it a linear ODE.

General vs particular solutions

The solution set is typically a family with $n$ free constants: \[ y(x)=y(x; C_1,\dots,C_n). \] Supplying $n$ conditions (e.g., initial values $y(x_0),y'(x_0),\dots,y^{(n-1)}(x_0)$) selects a particular member.

Example: exponential growth/decay

$y'=ky$ has general solution $y(x)=C\,e^{kx}$. With $y(0)=y_0$ we obtain the particular solution $y(x)=y_0 e^{kx}$.

DEs as ‚Äúrules of change‚Äù

A DE is often a compact statement of physics or dynamics (e.g., Newton‚Äôs second law $m y''=F$). In modeling, we write down the rule of change and then determine the family it implies.

Context & extensions

  • Linear first-order: $y'+p(x)y=q(x)$ solved by an integrating factor.
  • Second-order linear: $a y''+b y'+c y=f(x)$ ‚Äî characteristic equation / variation of parameters.
  • Systems: $\\mathbf{x}'=A\\mathbf{x}$ with solution $\\mathbf{x}(t)=e^{At}\\mathbf{x}_0$.
  • Stochastic DEs: dynamics with random forcing (e.g., Brownian motion) $\\mathrm{d}X_t = f(X_t,t)\\,\\mathrm{d}t + g(X_t,t)\\,\\mathrm{d}W_t$.
